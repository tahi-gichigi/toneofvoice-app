# What Will You Do When You Don't Need Money?

A friend asked me this at dinner last month.

Not in a philosophical way. Not in a "what's your passion" way. He asked it like he was genuinely worried about the answer.

We'd been talking about AI, obviously. About how fast things are moving. About how his company just automated three roles that took him four years to hire for. About how his wife's firm is doing the same.

And then he just said it.

"What are you going to do when you don't need money?"

I laughed. Mostly because I very much still need money.

But also because I didn't have an answer.

## The preview is already playing

Here's the thing nobody wants to say out loud: a lot of us in the first world already have our basic needs covered.

We have food. We have shelter. We have Netflix and a phone that's more powerful than the computer that landed humans on the moon.

And yet we're more anxious, more competitive and more addicted to external validation than at any point in human history.

McKinsey reckons 45% of US jobs are automatable within 20 years. Not just factory work. Finance. Law. The kind of jobs your parents told you were "safe".

Elon Musk says work will become optional and money will become irrelevant. Which is easy to say when you're the richest person alive.

But let's say he's even half right.

Let's say in 15 years, some version of UBI exists. The robots do most of the work. You get a monthly deposit that covers rent, food, transport.

Then what?

## The two things that happen

I think there are two paths and most people won't consciously choose between them. They'll just drift into one.

**Path A: You finally make the thing.**

The novel you've been putting off since 2019. The pottery. The music. The weird app idea you keep explaining to people at parties.

When you strip away the need to earn, some people will genuinely create. Not for likes. Not for sales. Just because they have to.

Japan already has a version of this. A whole generation reviving traditional crafts not because it pays but because it gives them something money never did.

This is the optimistic path. And I want to believe in it.

**Path B: You find a new scoreboard.**

This is the one that keeps me up.

Look at what's already happening. We've got teenagers with more material comfort than any generation in history, and they're destroying their mental health chasing followers.

We've got adults performing productivity on LinkedIn like their careers depend on being seen working at 5am.

When money stops being the game, status becomes the only currency left. And status games are zero-sum. For you to win, someone else has to lose.

We don't trade economic anxiety for freedom. We trade it for social anxiety. Which might actually be worse because at least with money you can count it. With status, the goalpost never stops moving.

Sound familiar? It should. We're already doing this.

## The question behind the question

My friend's question wasn't really about money.

It was about identity.

Strip away your job title. Strip away your salary. Strip away the fear that you'll run out of money if you stop grinding.

What's left?

And this is the bit nobody wants to sit with.

Would you still write if nobody read it?

Would you still build if nobody used it?

Would you still create if there was no audience, no metrics, no applause?

If yes, you're probably going to be fine.

If you hesitated, welcome to the club. Most of us did.

## A third option

I don't think it has to be "pure artist" or "status addict". There's something in between that I keep seeing work.

It looks like this:

You make things for a small group of people who genuinely care. Not a million followers. Maybe fifty. Maybe twelve. You build a reputation through contribution, not competition. The recognition comes, but it's a side effect, not the goal.

You write the essay because you need to understand the thing. You build the tool because the problem bugs you. You show up because showing up is the point.

It's less glamorous than either path. But it's the only one that seems to hold up over time.

## What I'm doing about it

Honestly, I'm still figuring it out.

I know I like building things. I know I like writing. I know that brand tone of voice is niche beyond belief but it's the thing I understand from first principles and I keep coming back to.

I also know that the weeks where I felt best about my work weren't the ones where I got the most done. They were the ones where I made something I was genuinely proud of, showed it to people I respect, and went to bed without checking my phone.

That's not a business strategy. But it might be an answer.

When my friend asked me that question, I didn't have a response.

I think I'm starting to have one now.

It's not "retire and paint". It's not "grind harder before the robots come".

It's: keep making things that matter to you, for people who care, at a pace you can sustain. And when the money part becomes irrelevant, you'll barely notice. Because that was never really the point.
